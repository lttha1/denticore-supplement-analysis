## DentiCore Ingredients: What's Actually Inside

The official label lists eight primary ingredients. Here's what each one is generally understood to do, based on their documented properties:

**Calcium** — The backbone mineral for tooth enamel strength. Calcium is well-established in dental science as necessary for maintaining hard tooth structure and preventing erosion.

**Iodine** — Iodine has recognized antibacterial properties and is used in various oral health contexts, including professional antiseptic rinses.

**Copper** — Supports connective tissue integrity (including gum tissue) and has antimicrobial activity. Some research suggests copper plays a role in reducing oral pathogens.

**Chromium** — Primarily associated with blood sugar regulation. The rationale here is that high sugar environments in the mouth accelerate bacterial growth; chromium may help moderate this.

**Chlorella Vulgaris** — A green algae with antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Often used in supplements for its detoxifying potential and nutrient density.

**Chlorophyllin** — A water-soluble derivative of chlorophyll. It's the compound behind the breath-freshening effect you get from parsley. Also studied for its antibacterial activity.

**Boron Citrate Complex** — Boron is linked to bone and tooth mineral density. Some studies suggest it supports calcium absorption, which would make it a logical pairing with the calcium in this formula.

**Shilajit Extract** — A resinous mineral compound from the Himalayas. It contains fulvic acid and trace minerals, and has been used in Ayurvedic practice for tissue regeneration and cellular energy support.

These are all real ingredients with documented uses — that's worth noting. The supplement's claim that this specific combination "oxygenates gums" and "cleanses airways of bad bacteria" is, as the manufacturer presents it, the proprietary mechanism. Independent clinical validation of the full formula as a system hasn't been published at time of writing.

## DentiCore vs. Just Brushing Better

A fair question. Here's the honest framing:

Brushing and flossing address surface bacterial load and mechanical plaque removal. They don't directly deliver minerals to gum tissue or provide systemic antioxidants that reduce inflammation at the tissue level.

DentiCore's angle — that internal supplementation can reach gum tissue in ways topical products can't — isn't an unreasonable hypothesis. Calcium supplements are routinely recommended for bone density. The logic extends to tooth enamel and gum tissue.

What's less established is the specific "airway cleansing" mechanism. The idea that bacteria from respiratory passages migrate to cause oral issues has some support in microbiome research, but whether this formula meaningfully interrupts that pathway hasn't been independently demonstrated in published trials.

So: probably a useful complement to good dental hygiene. Not a replacement for it, and not a substitute for professional treatment of existing problems.
